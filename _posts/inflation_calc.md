You know that feeling when you're reading something that doesn't adjust for inflation? For example, [this article](https://thewire.in/film/what-are-the-highest-grossing-christmas-movies) claims that "The Grinch" (2018) grossed about 10% more than Home Alone (1990). But, assuming that the bulk of the profits were made in the first year after release and that the values given are nominal, what happens when we adjust for inflation?

I am far too lazy to run to an inflation calculator every time this happens. So as such, I've developed a back of the envelope inflation calculator. The US CPI is currently double today what it was in 1990, which was double that of 1977, then 1964, then 1941. Before WW2 it's a little murky, with the 1930s deflation, but the next halving is about 1910. In any event, remembering '90, '77, '64, '41, and '10 (and 2020, of course) gives you a basic 2^n number to multiply a dollar figure by, to give a ballpark real value.

So if most of the sales that Kevin McCallister generated happened in the first couple of years after the release, it's pretty obvious that his film grossed more than "The Grinch" after adjusting for inflation. Again, please note that this is not a robust method of economic analysis. Think of it more as an easy way to annoy your friends by constantly adjusting prices in old movies or TV shows. Because everyone watching "The Queen's Gambit" was really distracted trying to figure out how much $5 was in 1967. The guesstimate of $40 comes surprisingly close to the [actual](https://converselove64.tumblr.com/post/633924116028833792/im-currently-rewatching-the-queens-gambit-again) $38.85 that this Tumblr post describes. Yay guessing!!
